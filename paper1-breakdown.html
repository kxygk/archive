SUPP: Breadowns in CDB Assumptions

Intro

The main points of failure for climate dipole bisection center around breakdowns in our simplifying assumptions. Some degree of breakdown in each assumption will always be present and should be assessed on a case-by-case basis.

(Note there is some repetition with regards to the main text)

A breakdown in assumption A1

This is caused by two types of regional climate configuration:

As we mentioned, the most common climate configuration is the single climate pattern (such as a region with one monsoon associate rainy season) that is then forcibly bisected. An example of such a scenario is explored further in SUPP Single Climate Region.

The other scenario of concern is the presence of tertiary climate systems. We have not yet been able to identify any non-cooccurring tertiary systems (ie. a climate tripole). We do however find climate systems that take effect in parallel to the main dipole. However, even after searching, we have found it difficult to find regions where such tertiary systems are very clearly visible. While we naturally encourage evaluating each region on a case by case basis, such systems have a tendency to be significantly weaker than the dominant dipole. For instance, in southern Thailand, there is both the short-period MJO system as well as the interannual ENSO. Both are not easy to spot in the raw observation. Nonetheless, such tertiary systems are know to us a priori, and we can be confident they will in some way be skewing the climate patterns. Through a careful look at the daily precipitation climate index, we do manage to detect the MJO as a intermittent index reversal. Indicating that while a weak signal, it can on occasion overwhelm the monsoon signal ( SUPP CDB at Different Timescales ). The affected observations likely constitute a small minority of the data. From the perspective of the climate bisection method, skewed patterns map poorly to the SV1 SV2 subspace. Using the error bound estimation extension these skewed observations show up as "high noise", thereby are expected to have little effect on the final average. While we do not present any concrete evidence, we surmise it has a negligible impact on the resulting SVs and final patterns.

A holistic framework for accounting for tertiary signals will be an area of future work.

A breakdown in assumption A3

Imagining a scenario with a breakdown in A3, we would, for instance, expect different climate-associated patterns between strong and weak summer monsoon periods. Climate systems are never truly linear, and our analysis of single-climate regions suggests that this non-linearity is detectable to some extent. See: SUPP Single Climate Region. However, the fact that observations lie mostly along the ratio vectors strongly suggests that A3 is a safe approximation. Climate intensity derived skewing, just like tertiary system derived skewing, is effectively treated as noise during reduction to the 2D SV subspace. Again, error bound estimation can give highly skewed (from the average) outliers lower weight - improving results. We can get a sense for the extent of intensity based skewing by denormalize observations before projecting on to SV1 and SV2 (as done in all shown plots). One would expect the climate grouping to drift off-axis based on the distance to the origin (the distance to the origin being a metric of climate intensity). Arguably in our case study the summer monsoon half does show a small off-axis trend.

the cluster of yellow/summer observations are not aligned with respect to the origin

From the perspective of climate dipole bisection, intensity-based changes in pattern-shape will likely has a negligible effect on classification and index generation. Strong vs weak patterns, if different, would be expected to be much more similar than the two dipole phases.

A breakdown in assumption A2

When climate dipoles don’t cleanly separate it is typically for the following three reasons:

the observed parameter is slow to change.

For instance, when looking at a monsoonal region, one typically sees a period of wind reversal. During such phases a pattern is hard to define. The observations made during switch-over can be of any shape. The shape can be transient and changing (ex: El Nino’s equatorial SST "hot tongue") and they are in no way mathematically constrained. That said, working with real data we find that, when projected on to the SV1 SV2 subspace, these observation have always mapped to the region between the two climate pattern mixtures (ie "in the wedge"). "Switch-over" observation are inevitable in any dataset, however in the case of extremely slow parameters, they can start to form a significant fraction of the observations. This is a very typical for parameters such as sea surface temperature. See: SUPP South China Sea SST.

the region under observation is too large.

For instance, a region’s southern/norther section may be under a monsoonal regime, while the northern/southern section has not yet been affected. For the Asian monsoon region, delays in onset and withdrawal can be estimated using Wang and LinHo’s (2002) helpful maps. From these grids we can approximately estimate that the seasonal switch overs in southern Thailand will happen over ~1-4 pentads. This value could be further minimized by minimizing the case study’s region. While we should always expect some switch-over observations - in large regions these observations start to leak energy in to lower singular vectors.

The SVs are effectively describing observations as the sum of static patterns. To describe dynamic "patterns", such as a recurring propagating wave, the decomposition typically degenerates to sums of phase-offset stationary waves. Such a decomposition is highly useful. They’re often explicitly used to characterize wave-like climate systems - such as the MJO. However, such sums-of-stationary-waves are fundamentally very different from the climate patterns consideration thus far. The stationary waves come in sets, where the waves can typically be phase shifted, preserving their ability to describe the wave propagation. Such systems at face value seem to complicated our view of climate and patterns thus far. Fortunately this class of system can be typically minimized by looking at a smaller regions. When a wave-like systems is viewed at fractions of a wavelength, the wave-like structures look once again like dipoles. As the wave crest and trough goes over a small region, we once again return to the familiar view of a positive, negative and transition state. This issue stresses the need for high density mesoscale data, such as the IMERG dataset. They allow us to select regions that are as small as practical. A region’s minimum size is dictated by two factors:

  • The regional topography’s ability to drive distinct patterns in each phase of the dipole.

  • The lower value between "number of observation" and "number of points/pixels" dictates the total number of singular vectors. This may affect the ability to segregate signal from noise.

Note that the case study was not tuned to a minimal region size - as our objective was to illustrate climate pattern extraction.

the temporal resolution is too coarse.

When we perform the dimension reduction on monthly precipitation averages we often find months where both climate systems are active. SUPP CDB at Different Timescales.

In the first two scenarios above, transition-associated patterns are of an unpredictable shape. However, in this third scenario, as an artifact of integration time, signals are being actually added together. This is a bit unusual, as we do not expect different climate dipoles phases to combine linearly. This kind of integration-time driven mixing is not indicative of any underlying physical process, but is an artifact of what is essentially instrument resolution. Our case study, when observed at the pentad scale, no longer exhibits this mixing. SUPP CDB at Different Timescales.

This issue stresses the need for high temporal resolution data, again highlighting the utility of the IMERG dataset.

If one is very eager to further refine the extracted pattern one may wish to build region specific heuristics. If one has special insight in to climate transitions (ex: there is some a priori knowledge that seasonal transitions happen twice a year) then one may try to find criteria for removing problematic observations from the SVD. We have not found such heuristics necessary, and caution they may mask other issues. (See the MJO signal in SUPP CDB at Different Timescales). By looking at the SV1/SV2 subspace, it seems evident that even in the ideal case, any adjustment of the mixing ratio would at best give an extremely modest improvement to the final patterns - while introducing problematic tuning parameters.
