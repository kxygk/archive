= SUPP: CDB at Different Timescales

== Introduction

In extensive testing, the method has not been found sensitive to the observations' time window size. Nonetheless there are some considerations to keep in mind. We will reexamine the Southern Thailand region using monthly, pentad and daily time scales 

== Original Case Study

.Duplicate of Figure 1
image::diag/krabi-monthly.png[]

This is the result presented in the original case study. The monthly time step was selected because the climate patterns can be identified by-eye in the original observations (subplot "b"). The intention is to allow the reader to convince themselves that the final patterns are representative of the regional climate dipole. Data was aggregated in to months by IMERG (the monthly aggregates are available on the IMERG FTP server on the first of every month). While providing a compelling visual story, the main issue with this view is that monthly time bins are too large. The bins cause "transition months" where both monsoon systems are present, making some months appear as a mixture of both climate systems (the green and purple "in between" observations). It appears as if both climate system cooccur during these months, when in reality this does not happen. A typically transition month is one or two weeks of the summer monsoon followed by one or two weeks of the winter monsoon (or vice versa in the Spring).

== 2 Year View

For visual clarity, the case study is reexamined using just the first 2 years of the data.

=== Months

.Two Years - Monthly
image::diag/krabi-2year-monthly.png[]

The 2 year window makes the monthly SV1/SV2 plane very sparse, as there are now only 24 points/observations to work with. Perhaps surprisingly, bisection still seems to extract clear climate patterns. However, there are not enough data points to clearly distinguish the transitional observations/months we saw in the original full 10 year case study.

=== Pentads

.Two Years - Pentads
image::diag/krabi-2year-pentad.png[]

Looking at the pentad scale, climate patterns are no longer clearly visible in the raw observations (subplot "b"). As compared to the monthly result, here the SVD has generated an inverted SV2 (subplot "c"). Notwithstanding the poor pattern visibility, numerically observation still classify very well (subplot "d"), providing a very compelling separation of the two climate systems. There is a distinct angular gap between the two groupings and Otsu's method has selected this for the angular classifier (red line). The SV2 flip has caused "Top" "Bottom" patterns (and indices) to reversed position - as compared to the monthly case. Such a sign flip is common and has no detrimental effects. The extracted patterns are nearly identical to the monthly view, but the final index provides a much more interesting higher resolution insight in to the climate systems.

The other thing to notice is that there are several not-insignificant misclassifications of summer pentads - which will be discussed shortly.

=== Days

.Two Years - Daily
image::diag/krabi-2year-daily.png[]

At the daily scale raw observations (subplot "b") are again indistinct, with no visible seasonality. However, the first two singular vectors are visually indistinguishable from those found in the previous pentad case. Similarly the final extracted patterns, bisection/classifier and averages all correspond closely to those found previously. However, the projections on to the SV1/SV2 plane has become a lot more chaotic as observation-level noise has made the dipole harder to distinguish. One would be forgiven to misconstrue the climate system (and our observations) as mixtures of the wedge's two angular "end-members". While a visually compelling interpretation, it would not correspond to any physical process.

Notwithstanding the noise, the summer (yellow) observations are generally at the top and the winter (blue) observations are generally at the bottom. A distinct exception to this is the peppering of yellow points in the winter half. Correspondingly, in the final climate index there is a distinct intermittent winter-monsoon signal/index during the two summer periods. These index reversals happen at regular intervals and are almost certainly caused by MJO induces reversals in wind direction - creating a temporary winter-like pattern  during the summer. For reasons that are so far unclear, the converse (summer patterns in the winter) seems to occur at a much lower frequency.

It's important to stress here that while the summer MJO reversal does look more like the winter monsoon, this correspondence is incidental. It's expected that MJO-associated patterns, as well as other patterns due to tertiary climate systems, have distinct shapes of their own. 

A more comprehensive handling of such tertiary systems will be the scope of future work.

From the perspective of the dominant monsoon-related climate dipole it's important to only note that such reversals constitute a small fraction of the total observations and that they likely do not substantially skew the SV1/SV2 dipole subplane. This is further suggested by the fact that SV1 and SV2 are substantially the same between daily and pentad levels. Where at the pentad level these patterns have been mostly averaged out. As a result, their poor mapping to the monsoon-dipole-dominated subplane should lead to larger angular errors 
link:./paper1-erroranalysis.html[(SUPP Error Analysis)])
and should allows the dipole averages to not be severely distorted by such tertiary systems.
